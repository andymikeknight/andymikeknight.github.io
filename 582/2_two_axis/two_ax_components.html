Three-Phase Circuit Components in Two-Axes

Power

Instantaneous power in an $abc$ circuit can be written as

\[ \begin{align*} p_{abc} & =v_a i_a + v_b i_b+ v_c i_c \\ p_{abc} & = v_{abc}^T i_{abc} \end{align*} \]

Converting the $abc$ voltage and current to $qdo$ we obtain

\[ \begin{equation} p_{qdo} = \frac{3}{2}\left(v_q i_q + v_d i_d+ 2 v_0 i_0 \right) \end{equation} \]

Circuit Components

Resistance

Considering a three-phase system with balanced resistance of $R$ (i.e. the same resistance $R$ per phase), we can write

\[ \begin{equation} v_{abc} = r i_{abc} \end{equation} \]

where $r$ is a matrix given by

\[ \begin{equation} r = \begin{bmatrix} R & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & R & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & R \end{bmatrix} = R I \end{equation} \]

and $I$ is the identity matrix. We can subsitute $v_{abc}=K^{-1}v_{qd0}$ and $i_{abc}=K^{-1}i_{qd0}$ to give

\[ \begin{equation} v_{qd0} = r i_{qd0} \end{equation} \]

Or

\[ \begin{align*} v_{a}=Ri_a & \quad \quad & v_q = R i_q \\ v_{b}=Ri_b & \quad \quad \Rightarrow & v_d = R i_d \\ v_{c}=Ri_c & \quad \quad & v_0 = R i_0 \\ \end{align*} \]

Inductance

Considering inductive components, we have two relationships to consider. Magnetic flux linkage is related to voltage through Faraday's Law and Flux linkage can also be defined in terms of inductance and current.

Induced voltage due to flux

In the $abc$ three phase system:

\[ \begin{equation} v_{abc} = \frac{d \lambda_{abc}}{dt} \end{equation} \]

As with resistance, to find $qd0$ realationships we can subsitute $v_{abc}=K^{-1}v_{qd0}$ and $\lambda_{abc}=K^{-1}\lambda_{qd0}$. However, we now have a derivative term, and the transformation matrices $K, K^{-1}$ are not necessarily constant.

\[ \begin{equation} v_{qd0} = K\frac{d}{dt}\left(K^{-1}\lambda_{qd0}\right) \end{equation} \]

can be sovled using the chain rule

\[ \begin{equation} v_{qd0} = K\frac{d}{dt} \left(K^{-1}\right) + K K^{-1} \frac{d\lambda_{qd0}}{dt} \end{equation} \]

definining the speed of rotation of the two-axis reference frame as

\[ \begin{equation} \omega=\frac{d\theta}{dt} \end{equation} \]

gives

\[ \begin{equation} K\frac{d}{dt}K^{-1} = \omega \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 \\ -1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0& 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation} \]

Therefore, the $qd0$ voltage induced due to a magentic flux linkage is given by

\[ \begin{equation} v_{qd0} = \omega \lambda_{dq} + \frac{d\lambda_{qd0}}{dt} \end{equation} \]

where $\lambda_{dq}$ is defined as

\[ \begin{equation} v_{qd0} = \omega \lambda_{qd} + \frac{d\lambda_{qd0}}{dt} \end{equation} \]

Writing out the $qd$ equations seperately:

\[ \begin{align*} v_{q}&= \omega \lambda_d + \frac{d\lambda_q}{dt} \\ v_{d}&= - \omega \lambda_q + \frac{d\lambda_d}{dt} \end{align*} \]

The voltage terms $\omega \lambda_d$ and $-\omega \lambda_q$ are dependent on the speed of the reference frame, and are sometimes calls speed-voltage components.

Flux linkage due to inducance

The flux linkage dues to current in an $abc$ system is given by

\[ \begin{equation} \lambda_{abc} = l_{abc} \end{equation} \]

where $l$ is an inductance matrix that may be diagonal (e.g. for leakage inductances) or may be a full mutual inductance matrix giving the mutual coupling between phases in a three-limb transformer or rotating machine.

\[ \begin{align*} l_{leak_{abc}} & = \begin{bmatrix} L_l & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & L_l & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & L_l \end{bmatrix} = L_l I \\ l_{mutual_{abc}} & = \begin{bmatrix} L & -\frac{L}{2} & -\frac{L}{2} \\ -\frac{L}{2} & L & -\frac{L}{2} \\ -\frac{L}{2} & -\frac{L}{2} & L \end{bmatrix} \end{align*} \]

In the above equations, $L$ is the self magnetizing inductance if only one phase of a three-phase system with mutual coupling is excited. It is not the magnetizing inductance measured when 3-phase currents are flowing.

Again, to find $qd0$ relationships between flux linkage and current we can subsitute $\lambda_{abc}=K^{-1}\lambda_{qd0}$ and $i_{abc}=K^{-1}i_{qd0}$.

In this case we obtain

\[ \begin{align*} l_{leak_{qd0}} &= \begin{bmatrix} L_l & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & L_l & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & L_l \end{bmatrix} &= L_l I \\ l_{mutual_{qd0}} &= \begin{bmatrix} L_m & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & L_m & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & L_m \end{bmatrix} \&= L_m I \\ \end{align*} \]

where $L_m$ is the magnetizing inductance that is measured in balanced three-phase systems, $L_m = 3L/2$.

These equations show a significant advantage of two-axis analysis.

  • Resistance in an $abc$ system is unchanged in the $qd0$ system
  • If the reference frame is non-stationary, flux on the d-axis induces voltage on the q-axis, flux on the q-axis induces voltage on the negative d-axis. The magnitides of these voltages are dependent on the speed of the reference frame.
  • Current on into an inductor in either the q- or d- axis produces flux linkage on its own axis, with no mutual coupling.
