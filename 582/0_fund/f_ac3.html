In a three phase power system there are three interconnected circuits. The voltages and currents in each of the circuits are phase shifted by 120° electrically from the voltages and currents in the other two circuits.

The phase voltages in a circuit may be written in time notation as

\[ \begin{aligned} v_a(t) & = \hat{V}_{\phi}\cos\left(\omega t\right) \\ v_b(t) & = \hat{V}_{\phi}\cos\left(\omega t -\frac{2\pi}{3} \right) \\ v_c(t) & = \hat{V}_{\phi}\cos\left(\omega t -\frac{4\pi}{3}\right) \end{aligned} \]

where the subscript \(\phi\) is used to identify that this is a phase voltage.

Writing the voltages as phasors (using electrial degrees this time, rather than radians):

\[ \begin{aligned} \vec{V}_a & = V_{\phi}\angle 0^{\circ} \\ \vec{V}_b & = V_{\phi}\angle {-120}^{\circ} \\ \vec{V}_c & = V_{\phi}\angle {-240}^{\circ} \end{aligned} \]

If we assume a balanced system (impedances and induced voltages of each phase branch are equal), the currents may be written as

\[ \begin{aligned} \vec{I}_a & = I_{\phi}\angle \left(\theta \right) \\ \vec{I}_b & = I_{\phi}\angle \left(\theta-120^{\circ}\right) \\ \vec{I}_c & = I_{\phi}\angle \left(\theta-240^{\circ}\right) \end{aligned} \]

Three-phase Circuit Connections

Up to this point the three phase system has been defined in terms of the individual phase legs of a load or supply (a, b or c). The power system line quantities can be found from the phase quantities, depending on the circuit configuration. There are two types of common three-phase connection, Y- and Δ. The relationship between component phase quantiities and supply line quantities can found mathematically graphically. In the system supplying the load, consider three lines (1,2,3) with

  • line-line voltages$V_{12}\, V_{23}\, V_{31}$
  • line currents $I_{1}\, I_{2}\, I_{3}$

From the supply line variables (shown in blue in the diagrams below), it is impossible to know if the system is connected to a Y- or Δ- circuit (shown red)

Y-Connection

From the circuit diagram it can be seen that

\[ \begin{aligned} \vec{I}_{1} & =\vec{I}_{aY}\\ \vec{I}_{2} & =\vec{I}_{bY}\\ \vec{I}_{3} & =\vec{I}_{cY} \end{aligned} \]
\[ \begin{aligned} \vec{V}_{12} & =\vec{V}_{aY}-\vec{V}_{bY} \\ \vec{V}_{23} & =\vec{V}_{bY}-\vec{V}_{cY} \\ \vec{V}_{31} & =\vec{V}_{cY}-\vec{V}_{aY} \end{aligned} \]

The voltage phasor relationships are plotted graphically in Fig. 4.

Considering \(V_{12}\) the line-line voltage is given in terms of the phase voltage by

\[ \begin{aligned} \vec{V}_{12} & =\vec{V}_A-\vec{V}_B \\ \vec{V}_{12} & =V_{\phi} \angle 0^{\circ} - V_{\phi} \angle {-120}^{\circ} \\ \vec{V}_{12} & =\sqrt{3} V_{\phi} \angle 30^{\circ} \end{aligned} \]

The rms line-line voltage and rms line current are defined in terms of the rms phase quantities as:

\[ \begin{aligned} V_{LL} &=\sqrt{3} V_{\phi Y} \\ I_L & = I_{\phi Y} \end{aligned} \]

Δ-Connection

From the circuit diagram it can be seen that

\[ \begin{aligned} \vec{V}_{11} & =\vec{V}_{aΔ}\\ \vec{V}_{23} & =\vec{V}_{bΔ}\\ \vec{V}_{31} & =\vec{V}_{cΔ} \end{aligned} \]
\[ \begin{aligned} \vec{I}_{1} & =\vec{I}_{aΔ}-\vec{I}_{cΔ} \\ \vec{I}_{2} & =\vec{I}_{bΔ}-\vec{I}_{bΔ} \\ \vec{I}_{3} & =\vec{I}_{cΔ}-\vec{I}_{aΔ} \end{aligned} \]

The current phasor relationships are plotted graphically in Fig. 6.

Considering \(I_{1}\) the line current is given in terms of the phase currents by

\[ \begin{aligned} \vec{I}_{1} & =\vec{I}_{AΔ}-\vec{V}_{BΔ} \\ \vec{I}_{1} & =I_{\phi} \angle 0^{\circ} - I_{\phi} \angle {120}^{\circ} \\ \vec{I}_{1} & =\sqrt{3} I_{\phi} \angle -30^{\circ} \end{aligned} \]

The rms line-line voltage and rms line current are defined in terms of the rms phase quantities as:

\[ \begin{aligned} V_{LL} &= V_{\phi Δ} \\ I_L & = \sqrt{3}I_{\phi Δ} \end{aligned} \]

Thee-Phase Power Flow

Instantaneous power flow in each leg of the load is the product of instantaneous voltage and current:

\[ \begin{aligned} p_A(t) & = V_{\phi}I_{\phi} \left\{ \cos\theta + \cos\left(2\omega t + \theta \right) \right\}\\ p_B(t) & = V_{\phi}I_{\phi} \left\{ \cos\theta + \cos\left(2\omega t - \frac{4\pi}{3} + \theta \right) \right\}\\ p_C(t) & = V_{\phi}I_{\phi} \left\{ \cos\theta + \cos\left(2\omega t + \frac{4\pi}{3} + \theta \right) \right\} \end{aligned} \]

and the total power flow can be found from the sum of the power in each leg. In balanced systems, the total power is constant as the oscillating components cancel,

\[ \begin{aligned} p(t) & = p_A(t)+p_B(t)+p_C(t) \\ p(t) & = 3V_{\phi}I_{\phi}\cos\theta \\ P& = 3V_{\phi}I_{\phi}\cos\theta \end{aligned} \]

i.e. in balanced three-phase systems the total power is three times the average power in one of the phases. Apparent and reactive power are given by

\[ \begin{aligned} \vec{S} & =3\vec{V_{\phi}}\vec{I_{\phi}}^* \\ S & =3V_{\phi}I_{\phi} \\ Q & =3V_{\phi}I_{\phi}\sin\theta \end{aligned} \]

Power flow in a three phase system can be defined in terms of either phase or line quantities:

\[ \begin{array}{ll} S = 3V_{\phi} I_{\phi} &= \sqrt{3} V_{LL} I_{L} \\ P = 3V_{\phi} I_{\phi} \cos \theta &= \sqrt{3} V_{LL} I_{L} \cos \theta\\ Q = 3V_{\phi} I_{\phi} \sin \theta &= \sqrt{3} V_{LL} I_{L} \sin \theta \end{array} \]

Summary

This page briefly introduces three-phase circuits, which can be connected in either Y or Δ configuration. The relationships between phase voltage and line-line voltage, and between phase current and line current are presented. The expressions for three-phase power flow, Apparent Power, Complex Power and Reactive Power are given.
