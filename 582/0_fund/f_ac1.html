Single Phase AC Circuits

AC Notation

Voltage and currents in ac systems are usually assumed to be sinusoidal. In single phase power systems the voltages in the system can be described as

\[ \begin{aligned} v(t) & =\hat{V} \cos \left(\omega_e t + \theta_v \right) \\ v(t) & = \Re \left\{ \sqrt{2} \vec{V}e^{j\omega_e t} \right\} \end{aligned} \]

where $\theta_v$ is the phase angle of the voltage and $\omega_e$ is the angular electrical frequency. $\vec{V}$ is a phasor defined in terms of the rms voltage. Unlike other areas (e.g. signal processing, communications) phasors in power systems are always defined in terms of rms magnitude, not peak magnitude. The rms of a sinusoidal quantity is given by

\[ V=\frac{\hat{V}} {\sqrt{2}} \]

and the phasor notation is

\[ \begin{aligned} \vec{V}=Ve^{j\theta_v} \\ \vec{V}=V\angle\theta_v \end{aligned} \]

Similarly, an ac current may be defined as

\[ \begin{aligned} i(t) & =\hat{I} \cos \left(\omega_e t + \theta_i \right) \\ i(t) & = \Re \left\{ \sqrt{2} \vec{I}e^{j\omega_e t} \right\}\\ \vec{I}&=Ie^{j\theta_i} \\ \vec{I}&=I\angle\theta_i \end{aligned} \]

The relationship between phasors and instantaneous values can be seen in the plot below. On the left are the stationary phasors $\vec{V}$ (red) and $\vec{I}$ (blue). In the centre are the corresponding rotating instantaneous complex vectors $\sqrt{2}\vec{V}e^{j\omega_et}$ and $\sqrt{2}\vec{I}e^{j\omega_et}$. On the right are the time domain functions $v(t)$ and $i(t)$ which correspond to the real (x-axis) component of the rotating vectors. Note that the instantaneous vectors and cosine functions have a larger magnitude than the complex phasors. In this plot $\theta_v = 45^{\circ}$ and $\theta_i = -30^{\circ}$.

In single phase systems $\theta_i$ is often set to zero and voltage phase angle is used as the reference for the rest of the system. Defining $\theta_v = 0$ the voltage and current in a single phase circuits are written as

\[ \begin{aligned} v(t) & =\sqrt{2} V \cos\left(\omega_e t\right) \\ i(t) & =\sqrt{2} I \cos\left(\omega_e t\ +\theta\right) \\ \vec{V} & =V \\ \vec{I} & =I\angle\theta =Ie^{j \theta} \end{aligned} \]

Where $\theta$ is the electrical angle between the voltage and current. Note that in all this discussin we usually use degrees to describe voltage and current angles, but must use radians when we are calculating angles in terms of $\omega_et$.

Single Phase AC Power

We can think of AC power as being delivered/absorbed from a source, a circuit component, or simply flowing past a point in a circuit.

Power flow in a system is the rate of change of energy at the point in the system where voltage and current are measured. Instantaneous power is the product of the voltage and current functions of time:

\[ \begin{aligned} p(t) = & v(t) i(t) \\ p(t) = & 2VI \cos\left( \omega_e t +\theta_v \right) \cos\left( \omega_e t + \theta_i \right) \\ p(t) = & VI \cos\left( \theta_v-\theta_i\right) \left( 1+ cos\left( 2 \omega_e t +2\theta_v \right)\right) \\ + & VI \sin\left( \theta_v-\theta_i\right) \sin\left( 2 \omega_e t +2\theta_v \right)\\ p(t) = & VI\cos\left( \theta_v-\theta_i\right) +VI \cos \left(2 \omega_e t+\theta_v-\theta_i \right) \end{aligned} \]

The last two expressions above provide two different ways to think about single-phase AC power flow. In the first, we have a term that is always positive with a magnitude that is a function of the cosine of the phase angle between voltage and curent, plus an oscillating term that is proportional to the sine of the phase angle difference. The final expression gives a consant power term, plus an oscillation term. These expressions are mathematically equivalent.

The interactive graph in Fig. 3 plots example voltage, current and power waveforms as functions of $2\omega_e t$.

From the above equation and plots, it can be seen that the power is comprised of two components, a steady value and a component oscillating at twice the supply frequency. It can also be seen that at certain points, the instantaneous power is negative. Negative power indicates that energy flow is in the opposite direction to the defined direction of positive current flow. To obtain the average power flow with time, it is necessary to integrate power over one supply cycle:

\[ \begin{aligned} P&=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi}p\left(\omega_e t\right) d{\omega_e t} \\ P&=VI\cos\theta \end{aligned} \]

Reactive and Apparent Power

It should be clear that there is a current flow and instantaneous power flow which is not associated with actual power transfer. This occurs when a circuit contains energy storage components including capacitors and inductors. With these components, energy is transferred back and forth between the system and the component each cycle, with no net power transfer. To quantify the current and energy flow due to these component we use the terms Apparent Power $S$, Complex Power $\vec{S}$, Reactive Power $Q$, and power factor. Complex Power is defined using the product of the voltage phasor and complex conjugate of the current phasor:

\[ \begin{aligned} \vec{S} & = \vec{V} \vec{I}^* \\ \vec{S} & = VI\cos\left(\theta_v-\theta_i\right) +jVIsin\left(\theta_v-\theta_i\right) \\ \vec{S} & = P+jQ \\ P & = VI\cos\left(\theta_v-\theta_i\right) \\ Q & = VI\sin\left(\theta_v-\theta_i\right) \\ S & = VI \\ S & = \sqrt{\vphantom{a}}\left(P^2+Q^2\right) \end{aligned} \]

Apparent Power $S$, is the magnitude of $\vec{S}$ which is the complex power. $S$ has units VA. $Q$ is proportional to the oscillating energy stored in the system each cycle, with units of VAR. (Note that Watts are reserved for real power flow, $P$, and mechanical power.) The relationship between apparent power and power is given by the power factor:

\[ {PF}=\frac{P}{S} \\ {DPF} = \cos\left(\theta_v-\theta_i\right) \]

The cosine of the angle between voltage and current is sometimes called displacement power factor (DPF) or fundamental power factor (FPF). In a system with only fundamental current, the power factor equals the displacement power factor.

Leading and Lagging Power Factor

Power factor is calcualted as the cosine of the angle between voltage and currents phasors. This does not give us all the information about energy and power flows in an AC circuit, as we can see that the Reactive Power changes sign dependig on the relative phase angles of voltage and current. In Figure 4, we can see that in the phasor diagram on the left, the current phasor is lagging behind the voltage phasor, in this case, we define the power factor as a lagging power factor. In the diagram on the right, the current phasor is leading the voltage phasor and we define the power factor as a leading power Factor

Complex Impedances

Impedance in an AC circuit is a combination of resistance and reactance:

\[ \vec{Z}=R+jX \]

where X is reactance and for an inductor

\[ jX=j\omega_e L = jX_L \]

and for a capacitor:

\[ jX=\frac{1}{j\omega_e C} = -jX_C \].

The relationship between voltage and current in an impeance is given by Ohms Law

\[ \begin{aligned} \vec{V} & = \vec{I} \vec{Z} \\ V\angle\theta_v & = I\angle\theta_i Z\angle\theta_z \\ Z & =\sqrt{\vphantom{a}}\left( R^2+X^2\right) \\ \theta_z &=\arctan\frac{X}{R}\\ Z\angle\theta_z &=\frac{V}{I}\left(\theta_v-\theta_i\right) \end{aligned} \].

Power factor in an impedance is defined by the R, X values of the impedance.. Figure 5 illustrates the relationships between impedance, voltage and current phasors, and power, plotting the power triangle for both inductive (lagging) and capactive (leading) impedances and power

Conservation of Energy

Each of Power and Reactive Power follow the laws of conservation of energy. Since Complex Power is made up of Power and Reactive Power, it also follows these laws. Apparent Power, as the magnitude of a complex variable, can not simply be summed to give power balance in a circuit.

\[ \begin{aligned} \sum P_{supplied} & = \sum P_{consumed} \\ \sum Q_{supplied} & = \sum Q_{consumed} \\ \sum \vec{S}_{supplied} & = \sum \vec{S}_{consumed} \\ \sum S_{supplied} & \neq \sum \vec{S}_{consumed} \end{aligned} \].

Summary

This page very briefly defines AC quanties for voltage, current and power. This includes power factor, complex power, power triangles and complex impedance. Note that for many cases in analysis of electrical machines, we will define the terminal voltage of a machine to be our reference angle and set $\theta_v$ to zero.
